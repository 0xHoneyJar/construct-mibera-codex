## Cultural Context

Tyr (also Tiwaz) is the Norse god of war and justice, but a very specific kind of war god, one defined not by bloodlust but by the willingness to sacrifice. His most famous myth involves the binding of the great wolf Fenrir: the gods needed Fenrir to accept magical chains, and the wolf agreed only if one god would place a hand in its mouth as a pledge of good faith. Tyr was the only one willing. When the chains held and the gods did not release the wolf, Fenrir bit off Tyr's hand. This is a god of war who understands war's true cost and pays it anyway, courage measured not in enemies defeated but in what you're willing to lose.

The correspondence between Tyr and Mars is preserved in the days of the week: Tuesday (Old English Tīwesdæg, "Tyr's day") maps to Mars's day in Romance languages (French Mardi, Spanish Martes). But the Norse interpretation of Mars energy differs significantly from the Roman one. The Roman Mars is a conqueror, a patron of the legions, a god whose worship involves victory parades and captured spoils. Tyr is the god who loses a hand to keep a promise. The difference is between Mars as triumph and Mars as sacrifice, and this piece chooses the sacrifice.

Astrologically, Mars governs Aries and (in traditional astrology) Scorpio. It represents drive, aggression, desire, and the energy that turns intention into action. The 144.72 Hz Mars frequency in the Cosmic Octave system captures this energy as vibration, a frequency that can be felt in the body as urgency, as the physiological state of readiness that precedes action.
