## Cultural Context

Maya stelae are carved stone monuments erected across the Maya world from the 5th century BCE through the 9th century CE, massive stone slabs, often several meters tall, carved with portraits of rulers, hieroglyphic inscriptions, and calendar dates. They served as public records, royal propaganda, and cosmic markers, anchoring the Maya Long Count calendar to specific locations and political events. The quality of stela carving represents some of the highest achievements of pre-Columbian sculpture, with low-relief techniques that could produce extraordinary detail and narrative complexity on a stone surface.

Balché is a traditional Maya alcoholic drink made from the bark of the balché tree (Lonchocarpus violaceus) fermented with honey and water. It was consumed in religious ceremonies, not as casual intoxication but as a ritual technology for accessing altered states of consciousness. Some researchers have proposed that balché preparations may have been enhanced with additional psychoactive substances, including psilocybin mushrooms or the venom of Bufo toads, creating a pharmacological bridge between the Maya world and the broader psychedelic traditions that the Mibera codex documents.

Terence McKenna's Timewave Zero is a speculative mathematical theory that maps "novelty" (complexity, transformation) across historical time, proposing that the universe tends toward increasing novelty in a fractal pattern. McKenna derived this theory from the King Wen sequence of the I Ching and associated it with the end-date of the Maya Long Count calendar (December 21, 2012), a connection that is ethnographically unfounded but culturally influential. Jonathan Solter's "I've Been There" provides the primary visual reference, a contemporary artwork whose psychedelic aesthetic bridges Maya monumental art and the modern ethnobotanical imagination.

The composition renders a Maya stela as a Mibera, the figure carved in the hieratic style of Maya royal portraiture, holding a cup of balché as a ritual attribute. The Timewave Zero graph appears as a crack in the stela, transforming McKenna's speculative mathematics into physical weathering, the passage of novelty through time rendered as the passage of time through stone. The visuals draw primarily from Jonathan Solter's "I've Been There," an art reference that bridges the Maya monumental tradition with a contemporary psychedelic visual language. The piece treats the Mayan ancestor not as archaeological artifact but as living tradition still generating new interpretations.
