## Cultural Context

Leila Waddell (1880–1932) was an Australian violinist and occultist who became one of Aleister Crowley's most prominent associates, serving as his "Scarlet Woman", the magical counterpart and consort role central to Crowley's Thelemic system. A classically trained musician, Waddell performed at Crowley's "Rites of Eleusis", a series of ritual-theatrical performances staged in London in 1910 that combined ceremony, music, and drama in an attempt to create genuinely transformative mystical experiences for participants. She represents the intersection of high art and occult practice, the violinist as ritual instrument, performance as magic.

The Thoth Tarot, designed by Crowley with artist Lady Frieda Harris between 1938 and 1943 (published posthumously in 1969), is one of the most influential esoteric tarot decks ever created. The Lust card (XI) is the Thoth deck's equivalent of the traditional Strength card, reimagined as a woman riding the Beast of Revelation, not domesticating power but merging with it, an image of ecstatic union between human will and cosmic force. The card's name change from Strength to Lust was deliberate: Crowley argued that the card represented not the control of passion but its full expression.

The Sigil of Baphomet (the official insignia of the Church of Satan, founded by Anton LaVey in 1966) and the Temple of Set (founded by Michael Aquino in 1975 as a breakaway organization) represent two major institutional expressions of modern Satanism, LaVey's atheistic, individualist philosophy and Aquino's theistic, Egyptian-influenced path. Leviathan, Lilith, and Babalon are figures from overlapping occult traditions: Leviathan from Hebrew demonology, Lilith from Jewish mythological tradition as Adam's first wife who refused submission, and Babalon from Crowley's Thelemic system as the goddess of sacred sexuality and the dissolution of ego.

## Visual Elements

The Leila Waddell-inspired figure holds a violin, placing musicianship at the center of the piece's occult identity, the artist as practitioner, the instrument as ritual tool. The Thoth Lust tarot card fills the background, Crowley and Harris's reimagined Strength card serving as the piece's philosophical frame. The Sigil of Baphomet and Temple of Set symbols layer the background with institutional markers of modern Satanism, while the Leviathan cross, Sigil of Lilith, and Seal of Babalon appear inscribed on the violin itself, three traditions of transgressive feminine/monstrous power carved into the instrument of art. The overall effect is of a piece that takes its subject seriously as a genuine spiritual tradition rather than dismissing it as provocation.
